# Wittgenstein-Style Word Explanation

**Principle**: Meaning is use. You don't teach a word by giving a definition — you teach it by showing when a native speaker reaches for it instead of a close alternative.

**User's explicit request**: "I don't want to know the Chinese meaning, I just need to get the point in English." Do not translate. Do not give dictionary definitions. Give usage + contrast.

---

## The shape of a good explanation

1. **When you use it** — the situation that calls for this word (one sentence)
2. **A concrete example** — one short sentence using the word
3. **Contrast with a close word** — why not "use" / "take" / "do"? what's the distinction? (one sentence)

Keep it under 5 sentences total. The point is memorable, not complete.

---

## Worked examples

### leverage

You use "leverage" when you already have something, and you want that thing to give you more power. "He leveraged his connections to get the job." Not "use" — "use" is about spending something. "Leverage" is about multiplying what you already have.

### resilient

You say something is "resilient" when it gets pushed down and bounces back. "The economy is resilient — it keeps recovering after each crisis." Not "strong" — "strong" is about not breaking. "Resilient" is about breaking and coming back.

### pragmatic

You call someone "pragmatic" when they care about what works, not what sounds good in theory. "She's pragmatic — she'll take the option that ships, not the option that's elegant." Not "realistic" — "realistic" is about seeing the world clearly. "Pragmatic" is about choosing based on it.

### underscore

You "underscore" a point when you make it impossible to miss. "The numbers underscore how bad the problem is." Not "emphasize" — "emphasize" is saying it louder. "Underscore" is laying down evidence that forces the reader to see it.

### curate

You "curate" when you pick a small set from a much larger pool, for a specific audience. "She curates the weekly reading list for the team." Not "choose" — "choose" just means pick. "Curate" means pick with taste, with a reason, for someone.

### compound

When something "compounds", each new amount builds on the previous total, not the original. "Small daily habits compound over a year." Not "add up" — "add up" is plain addition. "Compound" means interest on top of interest.

## Anti-patterns (do not do these)

**❌ Dictionary definition**:
> "Leverage" means "the exertion of force by means of a lever or an object used in the manner of a lever."
Why bad: The user reads this, thinks about levers, has no idea when to use the word in a sentence.

**❌ Chinese translation first**:
> "Leverage 的中文意思是'利用'、'撬动'..."
Why bad: Violates the user's explicit request. Also, it anchors the mental model in Chinese, which is exactly what the skill is trying to break.

**❌ Too many close-word contrasts**:
> "Not 'use', not 'apply', not 'employ', not 'exploit', not 'take advantage of'..."
Why bad: Five contrasts is zero contrast. Pick the ONE closest word the user would have reached for.

**❌ Long abstract explanation with no example**:
> "Leverage is about the asymmetric relationship between an existing asset and its capacity to generate disproportionate returns when properly positioned..."
Why bad: The user can't build a mental model from abstraction alone. The concrete example is what sticks.

---

## When the user explicitly asks for Chinese

Only if they say "give me the Chinese" / "what's the Chinese" / "中文是什么":
- Give the Chinese **after** the usage-based explanation, never before
- One line only
- Format: `(中文对照：XX)`

Example:
> You use "leverage" when you already have something and want it to give you more power. "He leveraged his connections to get the job." Not "use" — "use" spends, "leverage" multiplies.
> (中文对照：撬动 / 利用已有的东西放大效果)
